## Rotator cuff
The rotator cuff consists of four muscles: supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis.
- Function: stabilize the glenohumeral joint and enable rotation of the humerus.
- Supraspinatus: abduction initiation.
- Infraspinatus & teres minor: external rotation.
- Subscapularis: internal rotation.

1. Rotator Cuff ‚Äî Detailed Notes

The rotator cuff is a group of four muscles that stabilize the shoulder (glenohumeral joint) and allow complex arm movements.
They are often remembered by the mnemonic S I T S:

Muscle	Origin	Insertion	Action	Nerve
Supraspinatus	Supraspinous fossa	Greater tubercle	Initiates abduction (0‚Äì15¬∞)	Suprascapular nerve
Infraspinatus	Infraspinous fossa	Greater tubercle	External rotation	Suprascapular nerve
Teres Minor	Lateral border of scapula	Greater tubercle	External rotation & weak adduction	Axillary nerve
Subscapularis	Subscapular fossa	Lesser tubercle	Internal rotation	Upper & lower subscapular nerves
Functions of the Rotator Cuff

Maintains the humeral head centered in the glenoid cavity during movement

Provides dynamic stability to prevent dislocation

Supports overhead and throwing movements

Clinical Relevance

Most commonly injured: Supraspinatus (impingement).

External rotation weakness: Infraspinatus or teres minor injury.

Internal rotation weakness: Subscapularis tear.

Tendon degeneration increases with age.
